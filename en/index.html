Can this process be automated with AI? The 5-question checklist

June 2026 · 4 min read

Before investing in automation it pays to know which processes are good candidates and which are not. After dozens of assessments, we always use the same five-question checklist. If you answer yes to three or more, that process probably deserves automating.

1. Does it repeat every week?

Automation pays off through volume. A process that happens twice a year rarely justifies it; one your team runs every day almost always does. Calculate the monthly hours it consumes: if it exceeds 10, keep reading.

2. Does it follow rules, even unwritten ones?

If you can explain to a new employee how it works (“when the invoice arrives, check it against the purchase order and log it in the ERP”), AI can learn it. Exceptions are not a problem: they get routed to a person.

3. Is the information already digital?

Emails, PDFs, spreadsheets or a CRM all count. Today’s AI reads messy documents surprisingly well, so “having clean data” is no longer a requirement.

4. Does human error cost money?

Processes where a slip creates a cost (misbooked invoices, duplicate orders, late replies to customers) carry extra ROI: besides hours, you save errors.

5. Does your team find it boring?

It sounds anecdotal but it is the best predictor. The tasks nobody wants to do are the ones done late and badly, and the first where automation shows up in team morale.

AI agents in customer service: what they actually do in an SMB

June 2026 · 5 min read

An AI agent is not the frustrating chatbot of five years ago. It is a system that understands natural language, trains on your company’s real documentation and connects to your tools to execute actions, not just to reply.

What they do well today

Answering frequent queries in your brand voice at any hour, checking an order status in your system and telling the customer, qualifying a lead with the questions your salesperson would ask, booking meetings against your calendar and logging every conversation in the CRM with a summary. In practice, 60–80% of repetitive queries stop going through your team.

What you should not ask of them (yet)

Sensitive decisions without supervision, complex negotiations and anything where human empathy is the product. A good agent knows when to escalate to a person, and does it passing the full conversation context so the customer never repeats themselves.

The key: integration, not the model

The difference between a useful agent and a toy is not which model it uses, but whether it is connected to your systems. An agent that can check your inventory, CRM and calendar solves things; one that only chats merely entertains.

Where to start

Collect the 20 most repeated queries from the last month. That list is 80% of the agent design work, and is usually enough to estimate savings before building anything.

Inteligencia Aplicada · Models

GPT, Claude or Gemini: which model is right for your company

May 2026 · 4 min read

Every week a model comes out that “changes everything” and every week we get asked whether to switch. The short answer: for 90% of SMB use cases, all three big ones work more than well enough, and the choice matters less than three other decisions.

Where there ARE differences

GPT stands out for versatility and ecosystem; Claude, for long-document analysis and reliability in agent tasks; Gemini, for native Google Workspace integration and multimodal. If your case clearly fits one of these strengths, use it. If not, any of them will do.

What really makes the difference

First, context: a mediocre model with access to your real data beats an excellent one answering blind. Second, integration: being able to act on your CRM, email and calendar. Third, flow design: what runs on its own, what a person validates and how results are measured.

Our practical recommendation

Build model-agnostic. If your solution is well designed, switching models is a one-line configuration change, and you benefit from every quality jump or price drop without rebuilding anything. It is one of our principles: we work with the best models on the market without marrying any of them.

And privacy?

All three offer GDPR-ready data processing agreements on their business plans, and for strict requirements there are open-source models (Llama, Mistral) deployable on your own infrastructure. Data never has to leave home.

AI Resources · Glossary

AI without jargon: 10 terms in one sentence

Just enough to hold your own in any AI meeting.

  1. LLM (large language model). The engine: a system trained on millions of texts that understands and generates language (GPT, Claude and Gemini are LLMs).
  2. Prompt. The instruction you give the model; the more context and examples it includes, the better it answers.
  3. Agent. An AI that does more than answer: it executes actions (checking your CRM, sending an email, booking a meeting) pursuing a goal.
  4. RAG. A technique so the model answers using YOUR documents instead of just its memory; it is what stops it from making up your policies.
  5. Token. The unit models use to measure text (roughly ¾ of a word); providers charge per tokens consumed.
  6. Hallucination. When the model states something false with total confidence; mitigated with RAG, validations and human oversight.
  7. Fine-tuning. Retraining a model on your data to specialize it; almost never necessary in an SMB (RAG is usually enough).
  8. API. The door programs use to talk to each other; if your tool has an API, AI can connect to it.
  9. Open source. Open models (Llama, Mistral) you can run on your own infrastructure: data never leaves home.
  10. Workflow / automation. A chain of steps that runs itself end to end; AI contributes the steps that used to require human judgment.
AI Resources · Prompts

10 ready-to-use prompts for your SMB

Copy, paste and replace the brackets. They work in ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini.

  1. Difficult email. “Write a professional, empathetic reply to this email from an upset customer. Stay firm on [our position] without losing the customer: [paste the email]”.
  2. Meeting summary. “Turn these notes into minutes with: decisions made, tasks with owner and due date, and open topics: [paste the notes]”.
  3. Sales proposal. “Structure a proposal for [client] who needs [need]. Include context, solution, timeline, a price of [€X] and two scope options”.
  4. Job post. “Write a job post for [role] at a [industry] company. Friendly, professional tone, must-have vs. nice-to-have requirements, and a salary range of [€X–Y]”.
  5. Competitor analysis. “These are my 3 competitors’ websites: [URLs]. Compare their value proposition, visible pricing and weak points against my business, which is [description]”.
  6. Social content. “Generate 5 LinkedIn post ideas for [company/industry], each with an opening hook, a 3-point body and a closing question”.
  7. Sales objections. “My product is [description] and costs [price]. List the 5 most likely customer objections and give me a convincing answer to each one”.
  8. Simplify a text. “Rewrite this text so anyone without technical knowledge understands it, in half the words, without losing key information: [paste the text]”.
  9. Decision with pros and cons. “I am deciding between [option A] and [option B] for my business. Ask me the 5 questions I should answer before deciding, then analyze both options”.
  10. Improve a process. “Here is how we do [process] today: [step-by-step description]. Identify bottlenecks, removable steps and which parts an AI could do”.
